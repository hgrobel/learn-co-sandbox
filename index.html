Icelandic Culture

Mythology and Folklore

1) Female werewolves by the name Nattmara appear as skinny young women, dressed in a night gown, with pale skin and long black hair and nails. They terrorize the villagers by "riding" on their chest as they sleep and giving them nightmares. They were also seen as an omen of death, for if a dirty doll was left in the livingroom, someone in the family would soon die of disease.
2) Trolls are believed to be stupid, slow, and easily defeated by any brave committed mortal. They come in any form and dwell in dark, musty places like the bottom of lakes and caves. Their king is called Dovregubben, who lives inside the Dovre Mountains with his court.
3) The Dökkálfar, or dark elves, live within the earth and are very greedy and cunning. They are very powerful spirits closely linked to the seid magic. Most of them are female, and masters of seduction and trickery.

Food

1) Hangikjöt, or smoked lamb with herbs, is one of the most popular dishes in the country.
2) Svið is a traditional Icelandic dish consisting of a sheep's head cut in half, singed to remove the fur, and boiled with the brain removed, sometimes cured in lactic acid.
3) Cod-Icelanders consume fish caught in the waters of the North Atlantic Ocean and can be had all the year round. They eat mostly haddock, plaice, halibut, herring, and shrimp.

Famous Artists

1) Jóhannes Sveinsson Kjarval grew up as a poor fisherman, but later was trained by a professional artist and went to the Royal Danish Academy of Fine Arts to study impressionism, expressionism and cubism. He quickly became one of the most prolific painters in Icelandic history.
2) Ásmundur Sveinsson is known for his abstract sculptures of men and women at work that grew more indesciphrable as the years went on.
3) Einar Hákonarson is an expressionistic and figurative painter who's art changed the static state of the Icelandic traditional at the time by introducing a pop art style.
